# From Reactive to Predictive: What AI-Driven Maintenance Actually Looks Like in Practice

"Predictive maintenance" has become one of those phrases that shows up in every facility management conversation — often without much clarity on what it means day to day. Strip away the buzzword, and the idea is simple: instead of fixing equipment on a fixed calendar or after it breaks, you fix it based on how it's actually performing.

## Three ways facilities have historically approached maintenance

**Reactive maintenance** — fix it when it fails. Cheapest to plan, most expensive to live with. Downtime is unplanned, repairs are rushed, and costs are unpredictable.

**Preventive maintenance** — service equipment on a fixed schedule, regardless of actual condition. Better than reactive, but blunt: you might replace a part that had years of life left, or miss a fault that develops faster than your schedule anticipated.

**Predictive maintenance** — use real performance data (vibration, temperature, runtime hours, past fault patterns) to service equipment based on actual condition, not a generic calendar.

Most facilities today run a mix of all three, and that's realistic — not every asset justifies sensor-based monitoring. The goal isn't to make everything predictive; it's to apply it where the cost of failure is highest.

## Where predictive maintenance earns its keep

Not every asset needs this level of sophistication. A predictive approach makes the most sense for equipment where:

- Failure causes significant operational disruption (chillers, elevators, critical HVAC)
- Repairs are expensive or require long lead times for parts
- Failure patterns are gradual rather than sudden (bearing wear, filter clogging, refrigerant loss)

For low-cost, easily replaceable components, a simple preventive schedule is often more practical than building a predictive model around them.

## What this looks like without a data science team

Facility teams sometimes assume predictive maintenance requires an army of engineers and custom machine learning models. In practice, most of the value comes from far simpler mechanisms:

- **Pattern recognition in your CMMS.** If an asset has generated three unplanned work orders for the same fault in six months, that's a pattern worth flagging — even without a single sensor attached.
- **Runtime and usage tracking.** Equipment that's running well beyond its typical duty cycle is a candidate for inspection, regardless of what the calendar says.
- **Basic IoT sensors on critical assets.** Temperature and vibration sensors on your highest-value equipment can catch early warning signs long before a technician would notice anything by walking past.

The sophistication can grow over time. Starting with clean work order history and asset data inside a CMMS is what makes any future predictive layer — AI-assisted or not — actually useful. A predictive model built on incomplete, inconsistent data won't outperform a well-run preventive schedule.

## The real ROI conversation

The pitch for predictive maintenance is usually framed around cost savings, and that's real — fewer emergency repairs, longer asset life, less unplanned downtime. But the more practical benefit for most facility managers is *visibility*: knowing which assets are quietly degrading before they become the emergency that derails next week's plan.

That's the actual shift AI enables in facility management — not replacing the maintenance team's judgment, but giving them earlier, clearer signals to act on.
